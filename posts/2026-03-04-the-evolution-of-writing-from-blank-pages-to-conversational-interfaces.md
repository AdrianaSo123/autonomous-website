For a long time, writing began with a very specific moment. You would open a document, stare at a blank page, and try to decide how to begin. The cursor would blink at the top of the screen, waiting for the first sentence. Often, that moment was the hardest part, not because you didn't have ideas, but because turning those ideas into structured writing felt like a separate task from thinking itself. You weren't just thinking, you were performing the act of writing.

But that may be starting to change. As conversational interfaces become more common, the blank page is slowly disappearing. Instead of forcing yourself to type the perfect opening sentence, you can simply start talking. You explain what you're thinking, describe an idea, or ask a question, and the system responds. The starting point is no longer silence, it's conversation.

This shift matters because it lowers the barrier to beginning. The blank page used to demand clarity before you started. Now clarity can emerge during the process. Instead of struggling to begin, you simply start speaking.
